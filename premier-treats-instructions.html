How to Play

🎯 Objective

You are the supply chain planner at Premier Treats, a confectionery manufacturer. Your job is to decide how much to produce and how many raw materials to order each week — balancing service levels against inventory costs.

The simulation runs for 9 weeks (Week 0–8). Week 0 is pre-filled to show you the starting position. From Week 1 onward, you make the decisions.

Your goal: minimise total supply chain cost while keeping customer service levels as high as possible.

✏️ Your Decisions

Each week you enter four values:

InputWhat it means
Production OrderHow many finished goods (FG) units to produce this week. Maximum capacity is 1,200 units. Production is constrained by available raw materials.
RM1 OrderRaw Material 1 to order — arrives in 2 weeks.
RM2 OrderRaw Material 2 to order — arrives in 2 weeks.
RM3 OrderRaw Material 3 to order — arrives in 2 weeks.
💡 Tip: Leaving a field empty is treated as zero. You can enter 0 deliberately if you don't need to order.

📦 Bill of Materials

Each finished good requires these raw materials to produce:

For example, to produce 500 finished goods you need 1,000 RM1, 500 RM2, and 1,500 RM3. If any material runs short, production is capped at whatever the limiting material allows.

⏱️ Lead Times

All raw material orders take 2 weeks to arrive. An order placed in Week 2 arrives at the start of Week 4.

⚠️ Important: Plan ahead! You cannot get materials faster, so anticipate demand using the forecast.

💰 Cost Structure

Your total supply chain cost is the sum of three components each week:

Cost TypeRate
RM Inventory Holding£0.50 per unit of RM closing stock
FG Inventory Holding£10 per unit of FG closing stock
Service Failure£20 per unit of lost sales

Lost sales occur when customer demand exceeds your available finished goods. Service failure cost is the most expensive — but holding excess inventory isn't free either. The challenge is finding the right balance.

📊 Dashboard Tabs

During the game, four tabs show different views of your supply chain:

📊 Overview

Week-by-week summary of production, demand, inventory, and service levels. Your primary planning view.

🏭 MPS (Master Production Schedule)

Detailed finished goods view — opening stock, production, total available, customer orders, shipments, closing inventory, and lost sales.

📦 MRP (Material Requirements Planning)

Raw material detail for RM1, RM2, and RM3 — opening stock, receipts, totals, usage, and closing balances. Shows the maximum FG each material can support.

💰 Costs

Running cost breakdown — RM holding, FG holding, and service failure costs per week, with cumulative totals and a chart.

🎮 Game Rounds

Practice Round Easy

Learn the mechanics with stable demand and no disruptions. Use this to understand how production, materials, and costs interact.

Round 1 Medium

Same mechanics, but a supply chain disruption may affect your raw material deliveries. Stay alert to supplier notifications.

Round 2 Hard

Multiple disruptions test your planning resilience — both supplier delays and factory capacity constraints. Can you adapt?

⚠️ Disruptions: When a disruption occurs, a notification banner appears. Read it carefully — it tells you exactly what has changed (e.g., a delayed shipment or reduced capacity). You cannot undo orders already placed.

🏆 Strategy Tips

💡 Use the forecast: The forecast for next week is revealed each round. It won't be perfect, but it's your best planning signal.
💡 Watch the BOM constraint: RM3 is the tightest material (3 units per FG). Running out of RM3 is the most common bottleneck.
💡 Balance inventory vs. service: Lost sales cost £20/unit while FG holding is £10/unit. A small safety stock is cheaper than missing orders.
💡 Order RM early: The 2-week lead time means you're always ordering for two weeks ahead. Don't wait until stock runs low.
